Our Family

Rugby Football

Walter and Emma West had nine children, six of them were boys, three of them were very successful on the rugby field. Herbert was six years older than Thomas, and eighteen years older than Reginald. All the boys played at the same position, scrum-half, but Tom also played at outside-half (alongside Herbert in a few first team games for Bath) and, for the major part of his career, at wing three-quarter. Herbert sustained an eye injury, which probably limited his career, but Thomas played ninety first team games for Bath Football Club, including a season as captain of the club.

Herbert also played for a team from his workplace (Stothert & Pitt).

The next generation, grandsons of Walter and Emma, were also involved in the local rugby clubs. Herbert's son Cyril (Bud) West was a very successful full-back, though his playing career for Bath was interrupted by military service in the second world war. Buds cousins Arthur and Edward also played for the club as did in-laws Charles (Joe) Kent, who had been at school with Bud, and Ken Sherriff, who married Phyllis.

Until 1995, rugby union was an exclusively amateur game, so that players for local teams would be men living locally to the clubs and, most often, men who had learned the game together at local schools. This made the sport, and its clubs, a particularly strong element of many local communities.

Tom West

Tom started playing for the Bath Football Club first XV in 1903 when he was twenty years old. He was an extremely fast runner, having ‘won several prizes on the running track’. At that time the fortunes of the Bath team were patchy, they were often unable to field a full team. Many players would come to a Saturday match from working that morning and for away games they would rely on public transport, match reports show that players were delayed by missed trains on several occasions. However, it is clear from these reports that Tom was an outstanding player, playing half-back and three-quarter positions.

Towards the end of the 1906/07 season he was working in Gloucester and turned out for the local side. The report in the local newspaper of his first game read:

West. the ex-Bath three-quarter (who is working in Gloucester) made a highly satisfactory debut in the City ranks. Such a game was not a severe test, but West showed pace and cleverness in attack, and had the distinction of scoring four tries.
Gloucester Citizen - Saturday 3rd March 1907

He finished that season at Gloucester but returned to the Bath club the following season and was made captain. Results for that season were disappointing and then, at the start of the 1908/09 season he played just three games before moving to Lancashire to play for the very successful Rochdale Hornets club.

Rochdale Hornets was a founding member of the Northern Rugby Union (predecessor of the Rugby League), formed only thirteen years previously. The Northern Union allowed payments to players for time missed from work, meaning that clubs could consistantly achieve good results, and that players like Tom could take their sport more seriously.

The first world war was declared on 28th July 1914 and Tom volunteered early in the conflict. He was killed in action in September 1915.

The following is a quote from 'Bath in the Great War (Your Towns and Cities in the Great War)'

On Saturday 9 October [1915], the Bath Chronicle announced that Tom West, a well-known Bath rugby footballer, had died in action. His parents, Mr and Mrs Walter West of 17 Stuart Place, Twerton, received news on the previous Saturday night that he had been killed in action on Wednesday 29 September.

Rifleman Tom West had served with ‘A’ Company of the 10th Battalion King’s Royal Rifle Corps. The officer who wrote to tell his parents of his death said that he died instantaneously and remarked: He was a good soldier, a kind friend, faithful in his duty and will be missed by all.

Enclosed with the note was a letter that Rifleman West had written to his mother but had not lived long enough to post. It began with the message: I may not have the opportunity of writing to you for a little while, so you must not worry if you don’t hear from me. I shall be quite all right. We are keeping in good condition.

Tom West first played rugby for the Oldfield Park Club before joining the Bath XV. . . . West also made several appearances for Somerset. But in 1908, he forsook amateur rugby for the Northern Union, being secured by Rochdale Hornets. With them he played finely and assisted in winning the Northern Union Cup for his team.

He held a good position in the Corporation Electricity Works at Rochdale. This he threw up, and also his football, to join the Army last autumn, when he enlisted in the Rifle Corps.

He was in Bath at Christmas on leave and went to the front with his regiment only a short time ago. When at Aldershot, he met on the parade ground an old Bath football and running pal who had also enlisted in the same battalion. This was Bert Lewis, formerly captain of the Walcot rugby team and afterwards, a three-quarter for Bath. . . . It was a delightful surprise to the men to find themselves comrades and they had been together ever since. Lewis was at Tom’s side when he was struck down by a German bullet and has written home to tell of West’s glorious death.

Bath in the Great War - Tait, Derek
